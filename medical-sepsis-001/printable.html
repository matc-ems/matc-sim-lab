S Signs & Symptoms
75-year-old male nursing home resident with a baseline of mild dementia (oriented to person and place at baseline, ambulatory with walker) developed a fever 48 hours ago to a maximum of 102.6°F, with progressive lethargy, decreased PO intake, and one episode of incontinence today. Nursing staff noticed cloudy, foul-smelling urine in his Foley collection bag yesterday and again this morning. Per staff, his last set of vitals at 06:00 was BP 96/58, HR 108, T 101.8°F, SpO2 94% on RA. Now at 11:00 he is too weak to stand, only opens his eyes to voice, and answers only with single words. No reported chest pain, vomiting, or diarrhea. Tylenol given at 06:00 with no clinical improvement.

Initial Contact
EMS arrives to find a 75-year-old nursing home resident who has deteriorated over 48 hours from baseline ambulatory dementia to lethargic and unable to stand. He is febrile, tachycardic, hypotensive, and mottled at the extremities with flash capillary refill at the chest — classic distributive (septic) shock pattern. The Foley bag is full of cloudy, sediment-laden urine. The teaching point sets up immediately: recognize sepsis as the cause of his AMS and shock, give early aggressive fluid resuscitation with explicit mL/kg math, and prepare for vasopressor escalation if fluid-refractory.

Expected Actions
critical
Primary survey; recognize septic shock (fever + hypotension + AMS + warm-flushed/mottled pattern)
Inclusion Criteria: Signs of poor perfusion (due to a medical cause) such as one or more of the following: Altered mental status; Delayed capillary refill (> 3 seconds); Flash capillary refill (> 1 second) seen in early septic shock; Decreased urine output; Respiratory rate greater than 20 breaths per minute in adults. Pertinent Assessment Findings: Distributive shock: flash capillary refill, bounding peripheral pulses.

Improper Care Branch

Trigger: Inadequate fluid resuscitation and no pre-positioned vasopressor strategy; septic shock progressed to deeper shock with AMS; rescue with aggressive resuscitation now required.
