Expected Actions
critical
Scene safety; manual c-spine; primary survey (MARCH); recognize trauma + AMS
Primary survey (Use 'MARCH' algorithm): Massive Hemorrhage; Airway; Respiratory/Breathing; Circulation; Head injury/Hypothermia. Perform initial neurologic status assessment of GCS/AVPU and pupillary size and responsiveness. Assume concomitant cervical spine injury in patients with moderate/severe head injury.
↳ MATC: General Trauma Management
critical
Check blood glucose IMMEDIATELY — diaphoretic combative T1DM (BGL = 38)
Inclusion Criteria: Patients with blood glucose less than 60 mg/dL with symptoms of hypoglycemia. Patients with altered level of consciousness. Patients with seizure. Patients with history of diabetes and other medical symptoms. Patients who appear to be intoxicated. Pertinent Assessment Findings: Diaphoresis or hypothermia may be associated with hypoglycemia. ↳ MATC: Head Injury

Secondary survey: Head to toe physical exam including re-assessment of interventions from primary survey. Head/Face: Palpate head and scalp and face and evaluate for soft tissue injury or bony crepitus indicating injury to skull or facial bones.

critical

Monitoring: Initial neurologic status assessment and reassessment with any change in mentation. Eyes: Evaluate pupil size and reaction to light to establish baseline; Reassess pupils if decrease in mentation.
↳ MATC: Head Injury
IV D10 given; mentation improves
Trigger: BGL confirmed low; IV dextrose given (D10 preferred per protocol safety considerations); mental status improving; trauma evaluation continues with head injury and spine precautions.

Expected Actions
critical
D10% 250 mL IV (= 25 g) infused incrementally — D10 preferred over D50 per Safety #2
Unconscious patient, or patients who are unable to protect their own airway: Dextrose IV – administer in incremental doses until mental status improves or maximum field dosing is reached (if available, D10% is preferred). Maximum field adult dosing: 25 g of 10–50% dextrose IV: 50 mL of 50% dextrose; 100 mL of 25% dextrose; 250 mL of 10% dextrose. Patient Safety: Dextrose 50% can cause local tissue damage if it extravasates from vein and may cause hyperglycemia. Dextrose 50% carries risk for little clinical gain. EMS systems may consider carrying no more than 25% concentration of dextrose for treating hypoglycemia in adults.
↳ MATC: Hypoglycemia
important
Stop / disconnect insulin pump — known T1DM pump patient with hypoglycemia
For patients with an insulin pump who are hypoglycemic with associated altered mental status (GCS less than 15): Stop the pump, disconnect, or remove at insertion site if patient cannot ingest oral glucose or ALS is not available.
↳ MATC: Hypoglycemia
critical
Repeat blood glucose 5–10 min after dextrose; document response
Reassess patient: Reassess vital signs (pulse, blood pressure, respiratory rate, neurologic status assessment) and mental status. Repeat check of blood glucose level if previous hypoglycemia and mental status has not returned to normal. Key Documentation Elements: Document reassessment of vital signs and mental status after administration of glucose/dextrose/glucagon.
↳ MATC: Hypoglycemia
critical
Transport — does NOT meet release criteria (concomitant trauma, head injury)
If symptoms of hypoglycemia resolve after treatment, release without transport should only be considered if all the following are true: Repeat glucose is greater than 80 mg/dL; Patient takes insulin or metformin to control diabetes and does not take long-acting oral sulphunylurea agents; Patient returns to normal mental status, with no focal neurologic signs/symptoms after receiving glucose/dextrose; Patient can promptly obtain and will eat a carbohydrate meal; Patient or legal guardian refuses transport and EMS clinicians agree transport not indicated; A reliable adult will be staying with patient; No major co-morbid symptoms exist, like chest pain, shortness of breath, seizures, intoxication; A clear cause of the hypoglycemia is identified.
↳ MATC: Hypoglycemia
critical
Maintain spine precautions; transport to trauma-capable receiving facility
Disability: Spinal assessment and management, per Spinal Care Guideline. Transport destination specific to head trauma: Preferential transport to highest level of care within trauma system: GCS 3–13, P (pain) or U (unresponsive) on AVPU scale; Penetrating head trauma; Open or depressed skull fracture.
↳ MATC: Head Injury
critical
Avoid hypotension — target SBP > 110 in head injury; no hyperventilation
Moderate/severe closed head injury: Blood pressure: avoid hypotension. Adult (age greater than 10 years): maintain SBP greater than or equal to 110 mmHg. Patient Safety Considerations: Do not hyperventilate patients: Maintain all patients in EtCO2 range of 35–45 mmHg.
↳ MATC: Head Injury

Patient transported on the cot in spinal motion restriction with cervical collar, head injury precautions, oxygen targeted to 94–98%, IV in place, and continuous monitoring. Crew provides bedside handoff using AT-MIST framework: 54M, ~30 minutes ago, low-speed MVC, 1-inch forehead laceration with mechanism for closed head injury, hypoglycemia identified as cause (BGL 38 → 112 after D10), insulin pump stopped.

Improper Care Branch
BGL not checked; treated only as head injury
Trigger: Hypoglycemia driver of AMS not identified; combative trauma patient treated symptomatically (restraints/sedation) instead of correcting the underlying metabolic cause.
Crew labels the patient as a 'combative head injury' and does not check glucose. Combativeness is escalated rather than diagnosed — restraints applied, possibly chemical sedation considered. AMS persists. The forehead laceration is dressed, but the underlying driver of the AMS — a BGL of 38 — is missed and the patient continues to be hypoglycemic en route. This is the classic miss the AMS protocol explicitly warns about: 'Hypoglycemic and hypoxic patients can be irritable and violent.' Crew must rescue by checking BGL now and giving D10.
