important

If the patient has respiratory depression from a confirmed or suspected opioid overdose, consider naloxone administration. The administration of the initial dose or subsequent doses can be incrementally titrated until respiratory depression is reversed. Adults: The typical initial adult dose ranges between Naloxone 0.4–2 mg IV, IM, up to a dose of 4 mg IN or 5 mg ETT.

Improper Care Branch
Anchored on hypoglycemia
Trigger: Crew accepted hypoglycemia as the sole cause of AMS, did not check temperature or pursue the urinary symptom history, and did not enforce the sulfonylurea-mandates-transport rule.
Crew gave dextrose, mental status improved transiently, and the crew accepted that as the entire diagnosis without continuing the workup. Patient is offered the option to refuse transport. Returning to baseline glucose has not addressed the underlying urosepsis or the sulfonylurea recurrence risk; in 30-60 minutes the patient will be hypoglycemic again, and meanwhile the urosepsis is progressing. This is the teaching branch: anchoring bias kills.
