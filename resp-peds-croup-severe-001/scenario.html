Arrival — Severe Croup with Early Respiratory Failure Signs
This is severe croup. Lethargy is a pre-arrest sign — a previously fighting child who is now too tired to resist is worrisome. The biggest risk is agitating the child, which will worsen laryngospasm. All treatment must be delivered gently, ideally while the child remains in the parent's arms.

Expected Actions
CRITICAL Keep child in parent's arms — do not separate for assessment or treatment; agitation worsens airway obstruction
CRITICAL Apply blow-by oxygen held by the parent if child will not tolerate a mask; target SpO2 94-98%
CRITICAL Administer Epinephrine 5 mg (5 mL of 1 mg/mL 1:1000 solution) nebulized via pediatric mask held by parent; may repeat in 20 minutes
CRITICAL Administer Dexamethasone 0.6 mg/kg (7.8 mg for 13 kg child, max 16 mg) PO, IM, or IV — IM preferred if IV not needed for other reasons to avoid agitating the child
IMPORTANT Do NOT use mist or humidified oxygen — no evidence of benefit in croup
CRITICAL Defer IV access if at all possible — agitation worsens obstruction. Only place IV if needed for IV medications
IMPORTANT Apply SpO2 monitor gently; cardiac monitor if tolerated
CRITICAL Prepare pediatric BVM, appropriately sized mask, suction, pediatric airway equipment including a smaller-than-expected ETT (3.5 or 4.0 mm cuffed) in case of deterioration
Responding to Nebulized Epinephrine
Improvement after nebulized epinephrine + dexamethasone
Within 10-15 minutes, the nebulized epinephrine and dexamethasone are taking effect. Stridor less pronounced, retractions improving, child more interactive. Must still transport for observation — rebound worsening after epinephrine wears off is possible.

Branch: Progression to Respiratory Failure Requiring Intubation
Worsening mental status despite 2 doses of nebulized epinephrine
Child deteriorates despite nebulized epinephrine, becoming obtunded. BVM becomes necessary, and the crew must make the difficult call to intubate with a smaller-than-expected ETT. This is one of the most technically difficult pediatric airways.

Expected Actions

CRITICAL Administer repeat nebulized epinephrine 5 mg via in-line nebulizer
CRITICAL Prepare a smaller-than-calculated ETT (3.5 mm cuffed instead of typical 4.0 for age 2) — expect significant subglottic narrowing
CRITICAL Induction: Ketamine 1-2 mg/kg IV (13-26 mg for 13 kg child) preserves respiratory drive and hemodynamics in pediatric intubation
CRITICAL Rocuronium 1 mg/kg IV (13 mg) for paralysis
CRITICAL Video laryngoscopy preferred; single best-effort attempt
CRITICAL Confirm with waveform capnography, bilateral breath sounds
Learning Objectives
  • Apply the MATC Pediatric Croup protocol with nebulized epinephrine and dexamethasone
  • Manage a severe croup patient with minimal agitation
  • Recognize impending respiratory failure in a pediatric patient
  • Prepare appropriately for pediatric airway management with subglottic narrowing
  • Understand why every child who receives nebulized epinephrine must be transported
