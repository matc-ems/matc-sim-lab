ROSC After First Shock - Initial Stabilization
First shock at 200 J delivered, 2 minutes of post-shock CPR completed, then rhythm check reveals organized rhythm with palpable carotid pulse
After the single 200 J shock, compressions resumed for 2 minutes. At the next rhythm check the monitor shows an organized sinus rhythm and a palpable carotid pulse is detected. EtCO2 has surged from ~14 to 36 mmHg, consistent with sudden ROSC. The patient is comatose. The team transitions to the post-ROSC priorities: airway management, ventilation at 10/min (NOT faster), SpO2 94-98%, BP support, 12-lead, destination decision. Given the unwitnessed arrest, downtime ~8-10 min, and pre-arrest indigestion-like complaints, a STEMI is very plausible and a PCI-capable destination is indicated.

Learning Objectives
  • Recognize unwitnessed cardiac arrest with bystander CPR as still survivable when high-quality ALS care follows
  • Apply the cardiac arrest pit-crew model with defined roles and a code leader
  • Defibrillate as soon as the device is ready in unwitnessed VF arrests - no required delay for additional CPR cycles per protocol
  • Use the manufacturer's recommended (or highest) energy setting for biphasic defibrillation (200 J on Zoll X-Series)
  • Resume compressions immediately after defibrillation without a pulse check for 2 minutes
  • Recognize ROSC by multiple methods: organized rhythm, palpable pulse, EtCO2 surge
  • Apply post-ROSC priorities: airway, ventilation 8-10/min, SpO2 94-98%, BP support, 12-lead, destination decision
  • Identify Anterior STEMI on 12-lead ECG (V1-V4 ST elevation) and activate the regional STEMI system
  • Avoid the two leading causes of post-ROSC re-arrest: hyperventilation and untreated hypotension
  • Plan for stair/awkward extrications with mechanical CPR device readiness in case of re-arrest
  • Manage shock-refractory VF with continued shocks, epinephrine, and amiodarone, and consider termination of resuscitation or ECMO referral as appropriate
