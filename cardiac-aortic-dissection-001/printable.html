Initial Contact
EMS finds a 65-year-old man with classic features of acute aortic dissection: SUDDEN tearing pain radiating from anterior chest to interscapular back, severe HTN with longstanding poor control, heavy smoking history, and family history of premature cardiac death. Critical EMS exam findings: a measurable BP DIFFERENTIAL between arms (right arm 198/110, left arm 152/90 — a >40 mmHg gap), and a subtle left-arm grip weakness with intact right grip. 12-lead shows sinus rhythm at 92 with NO ST elevation and only nonspecific changes — this is NOT STEMI. The protocol pathway here is recognition of the pattern, OPIOID pain control (fentanyl preferred), CAUTIOUS BP reduction toward target SBP 100–120 to lower wall shear stress, GENTLE transport with minimal jostling, and routing to a CARDIOTHORACIC/VASCULAR-SURGICAL center — not the closest hospital. Aspirin is deferred or held pending physician guidance because this is not ACS and antiplatelet therapy can worsen bleeding if the dissection ruptures into the pericardium.

Improper Care Branch
Treated as STEMI/ACS; bilateral BP missed
Trigger: Crew failed to check bilateral BPs, pattern-matched chest pain to ACS, gave aspirin and nitro, and routed to a non-surgical hospital; en route the dissection extended with new murmur, clouded mentation, and propagating BP differential.
Crew anchored on 'chest pain = MI' without performing bilateral BPs and pattern-matched to ACS: aspirin 324 mg PO chewed, NTG 0.4 mg SL, and routing to the closest community ED (no cardiothoracic surgical capability). Within 10 minutes en route, the dissection extends — pain abruptly recurs, the right arm BP shoots up while the left becomes weak, the patient develops a new diastolic murmur (proximal extension causing aortic insufficiency), and mentation begins to cloud as cerebral perfusion suffers. The community ED, on receiving the patient, has to recognize the dissection on CT and arrange a critical-care interfacility transfer to a surgical center — a 60–90 minute delay in definitive therapy. The teaching points are: (1) bilateral BPs are the EMS-level discriminator that prevents this anchoring error; (2) destination decisions matter; (3) aspirin and antiplatelets in an undifferentiated severe-pain patient with risk factors for dissection can worsen bleeding if rupture occurs.
