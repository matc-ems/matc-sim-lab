Improper Care Branch
Rebleed with aspiration
Trigger: Crew did not position the patient to protect the airway and/or suction was not pre-staged. A third hematemesis event led to aspiration with hypoxia and worsening shock.
During transport, patient has a third large hematemesis. Because the crew did not position laterally or have suction immediately available, aspiration occurs. SpO2 drops to the high 80s, the airway is contaminated with blood, and the crew now needs aggressive SALAD-style suction, BVM with PEEP, and possibly an advanced airway. This branch teaches the cost of skipping anticipatory airway preparation in massive GI bleed.

Patient positioning can significantly impact respiratory mechanics. Patients with severe bronchospasm should be left in the position of comfort (perhaps tripod) whenever possible. Elevating the head or padding (shoulders, occiput) can assist with opening airway and respiratory mechanics. This can both improve the ability to ventilate and limit aspiration.

critical
Two-person BVM with PEEP 5; tidal volume just to chest rise
Use bag-valve-mask (BVM) ventilation in the setting of respiratory failure or arrest. Whenever possible, the patient's head should be elevated up to 30 degrees. Two-person, two-thumbs-up BVM ventilation is preferred. PEEP should be used with BVM. 5 cmH2O is generally an appropriate initial PEEP setting.
