Expected Actions
critical
Primary survey; recognize near-fatal asthma (silent chest + drowsy + speech 1–2 words)
Pertinent Assessment Findings: Severe respiratory distress may manifest with hypoxia, altered mentation, diaphoresis, or inability to speak more than 2–3 words. In the setting of severe bronchoconstriction, wheezing may not be heard. Patients with known asthma with severe dyspnea should be empirically treated, even if wheezing is absent. Key Considerations: Factors that have been shown to be associated with increased mortality from asthma include: Prior near-fatal asthma (e.g., ICU admission or intubation/mechanical ventilation); Prior admissions for asthma or repeated ED visits, particularly if in the last year; Heavy use of beta-agonist medications, or requiring three or more classes of asthma medication.

critical

Airway: Give supplemental oxygen for dyspnea to a target of 94–98% saturation. Escalate from a nasal cannula as needed to reach this goal.
↳ MATC: Respiratory Distress (includes Bronchospasm, Pulmonary Edema)
critical
Albuterol 5 mg + Ipratropium 0.5 mg nebulized via mask; repeat albuterol unlimited frequency
Inhaled Medications: While albuterol 2.5 mg nebulized is usually sufficient for mild wheezing without clinical distress, albuterol 5 mg nebulized (or 6 puffs metered dose inhaler) should be administered to all patients in respiratory distress with signs of bronchospasm (e.g., known asthmatics, quiet wheezers). Repeat at this dose with unlimited frequency for ongoing respiratory distress. Ipratropium 0.5 mg nebulized should be given up to 3 doses in conjunction with albuterol.

Trigger: First-line therapy delivered (DuoNeb + IM epi); IV access established; second-tier therapy added (IV steroid + magnesium) and CPAP applied with nebulizer in line as the patient stabilizes enough to tolerate it.
First-line treatments are flowing. critical
Do NOT jump to advanced airway in pure bronchospasm — risk of pneumothorax and collapse
Patient Safety Considerations: Invasive airways do not improve bronchospasm. The airway should be managed in the least invasive way possible. Supraglottic devices and endotracheal intubation should be considered only if BVM ventilation fails. Positive pressure ventilation in the setting of bronchoconstriction, either via a supraglottic airway or intubation, increases the risk of air trapping which can lead to pneumothorax and cardiovascular collapse.

Improper Care Branch
Escalation delayed → respiratory arrest
Trigger: Bronchodilator delivered alone without IM epi or magnesium escalation; patient progressed to apnea then arrest from hypoxic insult.
Crew started a single neb and waited for response without giving IM epi or IV mag. Patient progressed from drowsy + silent chest to apnea, then to cardiac arrest from hypoxic insult. Crew must now manage with BVM (gentle, slow rate to avoid air trapping), continue nebulizer, give IM epi, IV mag, and full ACLS if pulseless. The teaching point is that severe asthma requires PARALLEL escalation, not sequential.
