Initial Presentation - Suspected Beta-Blocker Overdose
Crew arrives to a 70-year-old male with altered mental status, bradycardia (HR 38), hypotension (SBP 78), and suspected metoprolol overdose. Time of ingestion is uncertain (possibly 1-3 hours ago). The presentation is classic for beta-blocker toxicity: bradycardia, hypotension, AMS, and the empty metoprolol bottle. The crew must apply the Beta-Blocker Overdose protocol: airway, oxygen, monitor, IV access, glucose check, fluid challenge, atropine (often ineffective in BB OD), glucagon (the antidote), and prepare for transcutaneous pacing if refractory. Activated charcoal is generally NOT appropriate here because of the time-since-ingestion uncertainty, declining mental status, and unprotected airway.

Atropine Fails - Glucagon Antidote
Atropine 1 mg IV given with no rate response after 3-5 minutes
Atropine 1 mg IV produced no rate response (typical for beta-blocker overdose - the protocol explicitly states 'atropine may have little or no effect'). The crew administers Glucagon 5 mg IV - the specific antidote for beta-blocker toxicity. Glucagon bypasses the beta-receptor blockade by activating cardiac adenylate cyclase via a separate receptor pathway, increasing cAMP, and improving inotropy and chronotropy. The crew must anticipate vomiting (common glucagon side effect) and consider ondansetron prophylaxis.

Learning Objectives
  • Recognize the beta-blocker overdose toxidrome: bradycardia, hypotension, AMS, sometimes hypoglycemia
  • Apply the Beta-Blocker Overdose protocol: airway, O2, monitor, IV, glucose, fluids, atropine, glucagon, vasopressors, TCP
  • Understand that atropine often fails in beta-blocker overdose because the beta-receptor blockade is not vagal-mediated
  • Use Glucagon 5 mg IV (may repeat to 10 mg total) as the specific antidote that bypasses the beta-receptor blockade by activating cardiac adenylate cyclase
  • Provide ondansetron prophylaxis with every glucagon dose to prevent vomiting and aspiration
  • Apply the activated charcoal decision rule: only within 1 hour of ingestion AND with a protected airway - this patient meets neither criterion
  • Differentiate electrical and mechanical capture during TCP using the femoral pulse
  • Provide sedation and analgesia for TCP, with reduced doses given AMS and hypotension
  • Recognize the need for early ICU/toxicology resources and notify in advance
  • Recognize that intentional overdose requires post-medical-stabilization psychiatric evaluation
  • Choose hemodynamically neutral induction (ketamine) for RSI in a shock-state patient
