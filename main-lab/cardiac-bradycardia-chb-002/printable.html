Initial Contact
EMS finds an 82-year-old man on the carpet, alert but pale and clearly ill. Initial monitor connection shows third-degree AV block — atrial rate ~85 with completely independent ventricular escape rate of 28. BP is 78/42 with cool extremities and mild confusion. The clinical picture is unstable bradycardia from complete heart block, likely worsened by his recent metoprolol dose escalation on top of age-related conduction disease. Atropine will be tried per protocol but is unlikely to work in CHB because the block is below the AV node — the teaching point is to PREPARE TCP at the same time atropine is being drawn, not after it has failed.

Improper Care Branch
TCP started without sedation
Trigger: TCP started without pre-pacing sedation/analgesia in an alert patient; capture achieved but patient agitated and at risk of dislodging pads.
Crew started TCP without giving any pre-pacing sedation or analgesia. Capture is achieved electrically and mechanically, BP improves — but the alert patient experiences each pacing impulse as a severe muscular jolt and shouts in pain. He becomes agitated, tries to remove the pads, and his wife is distressed at the bedside. The protocol explicitly states 'If pacing is performed, consider sedation or pain control' — this is not optional comfort care but a safety/efficacy issue, because an agitated patient who fights the pacer can lose capture and pull off the pads. The crew now must give analgesia and anxiolysis post-hoc and de-escalate while maintaining capture.
