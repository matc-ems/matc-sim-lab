She has been mostly resting in a recliner since discharge, getting up only to use the bathroom and eat. Her surgeon's instructions called for short walks every 2 hours, but with the abdominal soreness she has been less active than recommended. Her husband noticed her lips were 'dusky' and called 911. Her right calf has felt 'a little tight and warm' for the past day, which she dismissed as 'getting old.' She is a former smoker (quit 4 years ago, 20 pack-years) and takes no estrogen-containing medications. No personal or family history of clotting disorders that she knows of.

Initial Contact
EMS finds a 52-year-old woman 5 days post-op (laparoscopic hysterectomy) in obvious respiratory distress with classic PE risk factors: recent surgery, immobility, obesity, prior tobacco. Sudden-onset dyspnea, pleuritic right-sided chest pain, sinus tachycardia at 122, BP 96/62, SpO2 86% on room air, and a unilaterally tender/warm right calf. Lung exam is CLEAR — no rales, no wheeze, no crackles — which is itself a clue (severe hypoxia with clear lungs and risk factors for VTE = PE until proven otherwise). 12-lead shows sinus tachycardia with the classic S1Q3T3 pattern and an incomplete RBBB suggesting right-heart strain. The teaching point is recognition of PE pattern, supportive oxygenation, cautious fluid resuscitation, and rapid transport to a CTPA-capable ED — NOT nitroglycerin, NOT diuretics, NOT bronchodilators.

Improper Care Branch
Nitroglycerin given for 'chest pain'
Trigger: Crew gave SL nitroglycerin before working through the PE differential; vasodilation precipitated RV collapse and acute hypotension — preload-dependent right ventricle decompensated under nitrate effect.
Crew anchored on the words 'chest pain' from triage and gave SL nitroglycerin 0.4 mg before fully working through the differential. This patient's right ventricle is failing under acute pressure overload from the embolus and is critically preload-dependent — venodilation from nitrates dropped preload, the RV decompensated, and BP collapsed within 90 seconds. Patient becomes lightheaded, BP falls to 64/40, mentation clouds, and pulse becomes thready. The teaching point is that 'chest pain' is not a free pass to NTG — pleuritic pain with hypoxia, clear lungs, post-op risk factors, and right-heart strain on EKG should defer nitrates pending further workup. Crew now must support preload aggressively (cautious bolus + early norepinephrine), maintain oxygenation, and consider push-dose epi as a bridge while the drip is prepared.
