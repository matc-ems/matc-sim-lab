Expected Actions
critical
Identify pulmonary edema (rales, JVD, pink sputum) — NOT bronchospasm; NOT pneumonia
Etiology of respiratory distress: Bronchospastic etiology (e.g., asthma, COPD) is suggested by: Wheezing on auscultation; "Shark fin" waveform capnograph or prolonged expiratory phase; History of asthma/COPD. Fluid overload etiology (e.g., CHF, pulmonary edema) is suggested by: Jugular venous distention; Rales on auscultation; Peripheral edema; History of CHF, diuretic therapy, dialysis noncompliance, hypertension.

Improper Care Branch
Albuterol given; CPAP delayed
Trigger: Crew misread rales as wheezing and gave albuterol/ipratropium with delay of CPAP and nitroglycerin; pulmonary edema worsening, oxygenation falling, patient slumping toward respiratory failure.
Crew misread the coarse, low-pitched rales as wheezing and started a 5 mg albuterol/0.5 mg ipratropium nebulizer treatment without applying CPAP or giving nitroglycerin. The neb is a small mask the patient is barely tolerating, the bronchodilator does nothing for the pulmonary edema, and the underlying problem worsens. SpO2 falls to 80%, BP remains severely elevated at 224/120, and the patient becomes lethargic and slumps forward — impending respiratory failure. The protocol pathway is unambiguous: a CHF/pulmonary-edema pattern (rales, JVD, peripheral edema, CHF history, no wheeze) goes to CPAP + nitrates, NOT albuterol. The crew must now stop the neb, apply CPAP urgently (or BVM with PEEP if CPAP no longer tolerated), and give SL nitroglycerin 0.8 mg.
