Initial Contact
EMS arrives to find an 8-month-old, ~8 kg / 18 lb female on the couch in a pink onesie. She is pale, flat-affect, tracking slowly, with cool extremities and a visibly delayed capillary refill of 3 seconds. Breathing is fast and shallow at RR 60. On the monitor: narrow-complex regular tachycardia at HR 252 with no discernible P-waves — classic SVT for the infant age group (rate ≥220 with abrupt onset distinguishes SVT from sinus tachycardia). BP 80/52 (low-normal for age, but borderline given the clinical picture), SpO2 97% on room air, anterior fontanel flat. The student must perform the stable-vs-unstable judgment call: poor feeding + pallor + delayed cap refill + lethargy = SIGNS OF INSTABILITY but not yet decompensated shock. Pediatric SVT protocol: vagal maneuvers AGE-APPROPRIATE first (ice/cold pack to face for infants — NOT carotid massage, NOT adult Valsalva), then IV access, then weight-based adenosine. If truly unstable and no access, synchronized cardioversion 0.5–1 J/kg. The competing priority is to NOT waste time on inappropriate vagal maneuvers, and to NOT miscalculate the weight-based adenosine dose under the stress of working on a small, scared patient.

Improper Care Branch

Trigger: Crew uses inappropriate vagal techniques (adult Valsalva coaching of an infant who cannot follow commands, or carotid sinus massage which is contraindicated in pediatrics). Rhythm persists, time is lost, and the patient drifts further toward decompensation.
Crew attempts adult-style vagal maneuvers on the 8-month-old — coaching the baby to 'bear down' (impossible for a pre-verbal infant) or worse, attempting carotid sinus massage on the side of the neck. Improper Care Branch
Adenosine dose miscalculated
Trigger: Crew pushes an incorrect adenosine dose — either an under-dose (adult 'small' dose like 3 mg, or wrong patient weight) producing no conversion, or an over-dose (adult 6 mg into an 8 kg infant) producing a prolonged asystolic pause. Recovery hinges on re-doing the weight-based math correctly.
Crew miscalculates the adenosine dose. Two failure modes — both happen under the stress of working on a small patient. (a) Underdose: defaults to a 'kid-sized' adult dose like 3 mg or uses the wrong weight (e.g., assumes 6 kg instead of 8) — pushes too little, gets no conversion, and the rhythm stays at 252. (b) Overdose: pushes the full adult 6 mg into an 8 kg infant — that's 0.75 mg/kg, more than triple the 0.2 mg/kg max — risk of prolonged asystolic pause and proarrhythmia. In the simulation, the monitor shows either no rhythm change (underdose) or an extended 8-second asystolic pause with bradycardia to 50s before sinus eventually returns at 130 (overdose). Either way, the recovery is the same: recalculate from the patient's actual weight (8 kg), confirm the per-kg formula (0.1 mg/kg first dose = 0.8 mg; 0.2 mg/kg second dose = 1.6 mg), redraw the correct dose, and push it correctly. Crew leader should call out the math out loud and have a second crew member verify the syringe before push.
