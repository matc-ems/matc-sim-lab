Sequencing too rigidly costs the patient. This is a true competing-priorities phase.

Expected Actions
critical
Rapid primary survey; recognize florid cardiogenic pulmonary edema
Signs of distress include: Apprehension, anxiety, combativeness; Hypoxia (less than 90% oxygen saturation); Intercostal/subcostal/supraclavicular retractions, accessory muscle use; Grunting, stridor, inability to speak full sentences; Nasal flaring; Cyanosis.
↳ MATC: Respiratory Distress (includes Bronchospasm, Pulmonary Edema)
critical
Maintain upright/tripod position; do NOT lay supine
Patient positioning can significantly impact respiratory mechanics. Patients with severe bronchospasm should be left in the position of comfort (perhaps tripod) whenever possible. Elevating the head or padding (shoulders, occiput) can assist with opening airway and respiratory mechanics. This can both improve the ability to ventilate and limit aspiration.
↳ MATC: Airway Management (Includes RSI)
critical
Apply CPAP early — preceeds or runs alongside other Rx
Adults with suspected pulmonary edema due to acute heart failure or fluid overload (such as dialysis noncompliance): Restoration of adequate oxygenation and ventilation should precede or be accomplished simultaneously with other medication therapies below — CPAP/BiPAP: See Airway Management Guideline for goals of care and escalation of interventions. Non-invasive ventilation (NIV) should be considered early for severe respiratory distress or impending respiratory failure.
↳ MATC: Respiratory Distress (includes Bronchospasm, Pulmonary Edema)
critical
Cardiac monitor + SpO2 + waveform EtCO2 + 12-lead EKG
Monitoring — Pulse oximetry and EtCO2 should be routinely used as adjuncts to other forms of monitoring in patients with respiratory complaints. Continuous cardiac monitoring may be indicated in patients with respiratory distress associated with suspected acute or decompensated congestive heart failure (CHF) or dysrhythmia. 12-lead EKG may be indicated to assess for dysrhythmia or ischemia, particularly in patients with risk factors for coronary artery disease and/or presentation consistent with CHF.

critical

Patient Safety Considerations: The use of nitrates should be avoided in any patient who has used a phosphodiesterase inhibitor within the past 48 hours. Examples are sildenafil, vardenafil and tadalafil, which are used for erectile dysfunction and pulmonary hypertension. Also avoid use in patients receiving intravenous epoprostenol or treporstenil which are used for pulmonary hypertension.

↳ MATC: Respiratory Distress (includes Bronchospasm, Pulmonary Edema)
critical
Two-person BVM with PEEP 5 cmH2O; titrate up by 2–3 cmH2O
Use bag-valve-mask (BVM) ventilation in the setting of respiratory failure or arrest. Whenever possible, the patient's head should be elevated up to 30 degrees. Two-person, two-thumbs-up BVM ventilation is preferred. PEEP should be used with BVM: 5 cmH2O is generally an appropriate initial PEEP setting. Increase PEEP in stepwise fashion (2–3 cmH2O at a time) as necessary, allowing time for the patient to equilibrate with each change before further adjustments are made.

Improper Care Branch
NTG without BP recheck
Trigger: Crew administers SL NTG aggressively without rechecking BP between doses. After multiple stacked doses, SBP collapses to the 80s while pulmonary edema is still incompletely treated. Patient is now hypotensive with continued fluid in the lungs.
Crew correctly identifies pulmonary edema and starts SL nitroglycerin aggressively at 0.8 mg, but stacks repeat doses every 2–3 minutes without rechecking BP between them. By the third dose, the cumulative venous and arterial vasodilation has dropped SBP from 192 to 82. The patient is now hypotensive AND still wet — lungs are still full of fluid but perfusion has collapsed. Skin is now pale and clammy in a new way, mentation is fading, and the patient is suddenly cold and gray. CPAP at this point is removing what little preload remains by raising intrathoracic pressure further. The right response is to stop NTG, lay the patient back to ~30 degrees (NOT flat — she will drown), give a careful 250 mL fluid bolus, and prepare a norepinephrine drip while reassessing whether CPAP can be continued at a lower pressure or needs to be paused.

Patient Safety Considerations: Excessive PEEP over distends alveoli, increases dead space and work of breathing, reduces lung compliance, and compresses alveolar capillaries, reducing oxygenation and risking pulmonary barotrauma.
