As the child's HR drops in response to severe hypoxia, the crew reflexively administers atropine for the bradycardia rather than aggressively optimizing the BVM. The child continues to deteriorate and progresses to bradycardic arrest with poorer prognosis because the working minute was wasted on the wrong intervention. Recovery from this branch requires recognizing the error, abandoning the atropine path, and returning to ventilation optimization while preparing for arrest.

When the child arrested the crew correctly initiated chest compressions but failed to optimize ventilation. The BVM has a poor mask seal, no PEEP, no OPA, and the bagger is going too fast — no real chest rise. Compressions are excellent, drugs are being given, but the actual cause of the arrest (hypoxia) is not being treated. EtCO2 stays low. SpO2 stays absent. Without correcting the airway/ventilation problem, ROSC will not be achieved. This is the second-most-common pediatric arrest error: forgetting that pediatric arrest is respiratory in etiology and that ventilation IS the resuscitation.

Expected Actions
critical
Recognize ventilation failure — peds arrest is respiratory; fix the BVM NOW
Special attention should be applied to the pediatric population and airway management/respiratory support. Given that the most likely cause of cardiac arrest is respiratory, airway management may be considered early in the patient's care.
↳ MATC: Cardiac Arrest
critical
Reset the BVM: two-person, OPA in, jaw thrust, PEEP 5, watch chest rise
Two-person, two-thumbs-up BVM ventilation is preferred. Oropharyngeal airways (OPA) or nasopharyngeal airways (NPA) can be placed if needed to maintain a patent airway and make BVM ventilation more effective. PEEP should be used with BVM. 5 cmH2O is generally an appropriate initial PEEP setting. Ventilate with just enough volume to see chest rise, approximately 6–8 mL/kg ideal body weight.
↳ MATC: Airway Management
critical
Slow the bagging rate to 12–20/min with chest rise per breath
For children with an advanced airway, 1 breath every 3–5 seconds is recommended (12–20 breaths/minute). Avoid excessive pressures or tidal volumes during BVM ventilation. The goal is to avoid barotrauma as well as overventilation and related reduction of venous return/preload/cardiac output.
↳ MATC: Airway Management
important
Watch EtCO2 rise as ventilation improves — target ≥10 during CPR
Quantitative end-tidal capnography (EtCO2) should be used to monitor effectiveness of chest compressions. If EtCO2 less than 10 mmHg during the initial phases of resuscitation, attempt to improve chest compression quality.
