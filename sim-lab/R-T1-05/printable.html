Expected Actions
critical
Primary survey (ABC); recognize respiratory distress with suspected pulmonary infection
Patient Care Goals: Assure adequate oxygenation and ventilation. Recognize impending respiratory failure. Promptly identify and intervene for patients who require escalation of therapy. Inclusion Criteria: Patients aged 2 and older with respiratory distress due to disease processes including: Wheezing/bronchospasm from suspected pulmonary infection (e.g., pneumonia, acute bronchitis).
↳ MATC: Respiratory Distress (includes Bronchospasm, Pulmonary Edema)
critical

History: Onset of symptoms. Concurrent symptoms (e.g., fever, cough, rhinorrhea, tongue/lip swelling, rash, labored breathing, foreign body aspiration). Sick contacts. Treatments prior to EMS: Oxygen, inhaler, nebulizer, other treatments, chronic or recent steroids. Hospitalizations: Number of emergency department visits in the past year, number of hospital admissions in the past year, number of ICU admissions (ever), previously intubated (ever).

↳ MATC: Respiratory Distress (includes Bronchospasm, Pulmonary Edema)
critical
Recognize early sepsis: suspected infection + ≥2 of fever, HR>100, RR>20, AMS, hypotension
Inclusion Criteria: Signs of poor perfusion (due to a medical cause) such as one or more of the following: Altered mental status. Respiratory rate greater than 20 breaths per minute in adults or elevated in children. Hypotension for age (lowest acceptable systolic blood pressure in mmHg): Greater than 10 years old: 90. Tachycardia or bradycardia for age, out of proportion to temperature. Potential etiologies of shock: Distributive (sepsis, anaphylaxis, neurogenic, overdose, endocrine). Key Considerations: In most adults, tachycardia is the first sign of compensated shock, and may persist for hours.

Continuous cardiac monitoring may be indicated in patients with respiratory distress associated with suspected acute or decompensated congestive heart failure (CHF) or dysrhythmia.

↳ MATC: Shock
critical

↳ MATC: Shock
important
Position patient semi-upright (Fowler's/position of comfort) — supports respiratory mechanics
Patient positioning can significantly impact respiratory mechanics. Patients with severe bronchospasm should be left in the position of comfort (perhaps tripod) whenever possible. Elevating the head or padding (shoulders, occiput) can assist with opening airway and respiratory mechanics. This can both improve the ability to ventilate and limit aspiration.

Improper Care Branch
Pneumonia-only; sepsis missed
Trigger: Crew narrowed on pneumonia from the productive cough and crackles, applied high-flow oxygen targeting 100% SpO2, did not perform a sepsis screen, did not establish IV access, and did not give a fluid bolus. Patient's borderline BP has now dropped and AMS is recurring — uncompensated shock is emerging because the perfusion problem was unrecognized and untreated.
Crew anchored on 'pneumonia' from the productive cough, fever, and crackles, applied a non-rebreather at 15 L/min to push the SpO2 to 100%, did not check or did not act on the temperature/HR/RR/BP pattern as sepsis criteria, did not establish IV access, and did not give a fluid bolus.
