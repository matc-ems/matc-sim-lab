Attempt to keep the patient in the horizontal position, especially limiting motion of the extremities to avoid increasing return of cold blood to the heart. ↳ MATC: Hypothermia/Cold Exposure
critical
ABC sequence (not CAB) — airway first, then 5 rescue breaths, THEN compressions
Primary survey should include aggressive airway management and restoration of adequate oxygenation and ventilation. Unlike the CAB strategy used in standard cardiac arrest, patients suffering cardiac arrest from drowning require an ABCs (Airway, Breathing, Circulation) approach with prompt airway management and supplemental breathing. Initiate 5 rescue breaths followed by 30 chest compressions. After the initial 5 breaths, use ratio of 30 compressions to 2 breaths.

↳ MATC: Hypothermia/Cold Exposure
critical
Obtain core temperature (rectal or epitympanic) — categorize hypothermia severity
If possible and reliable, EMS clinicians should perform core body temperature measurements and categorize patients into one of the three follow levels of hypothermia: Mild: 32.1–35°C/89.8–95°F; Moderate: 28.1–32°C/82.5–89.7°F; Severe: 24–28°C/75.2–82.4°F; Profound: less than 24°C (75.2°F). Rectal temperatures may also be used, with caution to avoid worsening the hypothermia by undressing the patient (e.g., done in a warm environment such as a heated ambulance). If neither esophageal nor rectal thermometers are available, an epitympanic field thermometer with an isolating ear cap may be used.

The temperature at which defibrillation should first be attempted in the severely hypothermic cardiac arrest victim and the number of defibrillation attempts is unclear. Per the American Heart Association (AHA), if the patient has a shockable rhythm (VF/VT), defibrillation should be attempted. The state of Alaska's 2014 guidance advises that defibrillation should be attempted once, followed by 2 minutes of chest compressions, then rhythm and pulse checks. ↳ MATC: Hypothermia/Cold Exposure

Continue resuscitation — 'not dead until warm and dead'; transport to ECMO-capable facility
Patients with severe hypothermia and arrest may benefit from resuscitation even after prolonged downtime, and survival with intact neurologic function has been observed even after prolonged resuscitation. Patients should not be considered deceased until rewarming has been attempted. Transport as soon as possible to a hospital capable of resuscitation. If cardiac arrest develops consider transport to a center capable of extracorporeal circulation (ECMO) or cardiopulmonary bypass (if feasible).

Improper Care Branch
Standard ACLS — terminated early
Trigger: Crew ran normothermic ACLS spacing (epi q3–5 min, stacked shocks q2 min), did not measure core temperature, did not commit to active rewarming (wet clothes remained, no heat packs, ambient cabin), and called medical control for termination after ~20 minutes citing 'asystole' — without recognizing that asystole at low core temp in a submersion victim is not a death determination.
BRANCH (judgment error): The crew ran textbook normothermic ACLS — stacked shocks every 2 minutes, epinephrine 1 mg every 3–5 minutes at standard intervals, no doubled spacing for the cold patient, no core temperature obtained, no aggressive rewarming, wet clothing left in place. After approximately 20 minutes with the rhythm degrading from VF to fine VF to apparent asystole, the medic called medical control for termination of resuscitation, citing 'asystole 20 minutes, no ROSC.' This is the most dangerous failure mode in hypothermic arrest: the patient is not actually dead. Hypothermia protocol explicitly states a patient cannot be declared dead until rewarming has been attempted, AND apparent asystole at low core temp may simply be a profoundly slow rhythm that the monitor cannot resolve. The teaching beat: 'not dead until warm and dead' is a literal protocol rule, not a slogan.

critical
RECOVERY: stop standard-spaced epi; either withhold or double intervals until core >30°C → 35°C
There is little evidence to guide use of medications in severe hypothermia with cardiac arrest, however 2010 AHA updates recommend use of vasopressors according to standard ACLS protocols while the 2014 Alaska guidelines and the Wilderness Medical Society advise medications should be withheld until the patient's core temperature is greater than 30°C (86°F). Above 30°C (86°F), intervals between medication provision should be doubled until the patient reaches 35°C (95°F).
