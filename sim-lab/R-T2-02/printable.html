The temptation is to crank up the oxygen because '91% looks low' — but his real problem is CO2 retention/ventilatory failure, not hypoxia. He needs ventilatory support (NIV/CPAP) with bronchodilators in-line, not more O2.

critical

Oxygen is a drug with an appropriate dose range and undesirable effects from both too much and too little supplementation. Effective oxygenation meets the oxygen saturation (SpO2) target set for that specific patient in the context of their acute and chronic medical condition(s). Permissive hypoxia (SPO2 ≥ 90%) may be appropriate in patients with COPD or other complex respiratory pathology.
↳ MATC: Airway Management
critical
Initiate CPAP for severe respiratory distress / impending failure; coach patient
Non-invasive ventilation (NIV) should be considered early for severe respiratory distress or impending respiratory failure. NIV options include continuous positive airway pressure (CPAP), bilevel positive airway pressure (BiPAP), bilevel nasal CPAP, and high flow oxygen by nasal cannula (HFNC). The key to successful use of NIV in a patient who has not used it before is coaching and explanation of the process and reassurance of the patient.
↳ MATC: Airway Management
critical

If indicated, bronchodilators should be administered in line with NIV. While albuterol 2.5 mg nebulized is usually sufficient for mild wheezing without clinical distress, albuterol 5 mg nebulized (or 6 puffs metered dose inhaler) should be administered to all patients in respiratory distress with signs of bronchospasm (e.g., known asthmatics, quiet wheezers). Improper Care Branch
Over-oxygenated, narcotizing
Trigger: Crew applied 15 L/min via non-rebreather chasing an SpO2 of 91%, not recognizing the patient as a CO2 retainer with a baseline SpO2 of 88-92%. SpO2 normalized to 99% — but hypoxic respiratory drive was blunted, ventilation deteriorated, EtCO2 climbed and mental status declined into hypercapnic narcosis.
Crew misread the 91% SpO2 as primary hypoxia and slapped a non-rebreather at 15 L/min onto the patient. SpO2 quickly climbs to 99% — looks great on the screen. But the patient's hypoxic respiratory drive (which had been compensating for chronically high CO2) is now blunted. Over the next 4-6 minutes ventilation deteriorates: RR drops from 28 to 16 (looks reassuring but is actually hypoventilation), EtCO2 climbs from 62 → 72 → 80. The patient becomes progressively drowsier — GCS slides from 14 to 11 to 9. This is classic hypercapnic narcosis (CO2 narcosis), and the crew was misled by an SpO2 number that 'looked normal.' Recovery requires recognizing the trap: drop the FiO2 back toward baseline, initiate NIV urgently (ventilatory support, not more O2), and prepare for BVM/intubation if NIV fails.
