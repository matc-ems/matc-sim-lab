Improper Care Branch
NTG given without screening
Trigger: Crew gave aspirin and sublingual nitroglycerin reflexively to a 'chest pain' complaint without completing OPQRST, without palpating the chest wall, and without recognizing the clean 12-lead and benign clinical pattern. The vasodilatory effect of NTG in a euvolemic young patient with no underlying ischemia produces symptomatic hypotension.
Crew anchored on the word 'chest pain' from dispatch and walked into the apartment running the full ACS bundle in their heads. They put the patient on the monitor, gave chewable aspirin 324 mg, and pushed sublingual NTG 0.4 mg — all before they ever did an OPQRST, palpated the chest wall, or really looked at the 12-lead (which is clean). Within 3 minutes the patient becomes lightheaded and pale, BP drops to 84/52, HR climbs to 104. He says 'I feel really weird now, way worse than before.' Crew must now recognize they treated a non-cardiac pain with a vasodilator, lay the patient supine, bolus IV fluids, and reassess. On reassessment they finally palpate the chest wall and reproduce the pain — the true diagnosis was sitting there the whole time.

Improper Care Branch
Tunnel vision on ACS
Trigger: Crew applied the ACS bundle reflexively to a 'chest pain' complaint without doing a real OPQRST, without palpating the chest wall, and without exploring the recent URI / lifting history. The true non-cardiac etiology is missed; the handoff to the ED is therefore inaccurate.
Crew completes the standard ACS bundle — monitor, 12-lead, aspirin 324 mg, IV access — but never palpates the chest wall, never specifically asks about recent URI or heavy lifting, and never clarifies whether the pain is pleuritic or reproducible. The 12-lead is clean and the patient's vitals stay normal, but the crew never actually rules in or rules out a non-cardiac etiology. They transport as 'rule-out ACS' and hand off a vague 'young guy with chest pain, clean 12-lead, aspirin on board' report. The patient leaves the encounter with an unnecessary aspirin dose, an unnecessary IV, an undiagnosed musculoskeletal/pleuritic pain, and no real reassurance — and the ED begins a chest-pain rule-out pathway that the EMS assessment could have headed off. The 'patient harm' here is subtle: not a hypotensive event, but an incomplete assessment and a misleading handoff.
