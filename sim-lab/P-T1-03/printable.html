This is a Tier 1 assessment scenario — the goal is to step through the Pediatric Assessment Triangle (PAT) from the doorway, then put hands on for a focused exam (vitals, auscultation, hydration, feeding history) BEFORE choosing an intervention. The right interventions are gentle — nasal suctioning and supplemental O2 — not a reflexive nebulizer.

important
Position infant in mom's arms / position of comfort; minimize handling
Patient positioning can significantly impact respiratory mechanics. Patients with severe bronchospasm should be left in the position of comfort (perhaps tripod) whenever possible. Elevating the head or padding (shoulders, occiput) can assist with opening airway and respiratory mechanics. This can both improve the ability to ventilate and limit aspiration.

critical
Do NOT give routine albuterol; bronchiolitis does not respond to it
Key Considerations: Although albuterol and steroids have previously been a consideration, the most recent evidence does not demonstrate a benefit in routine use of albuterol or steroids for bronchiolitis. Ipratropium and other anticholinergic agents should not be given to children with bronchiolitis in the prehospital setting. Steroids are not efficacious and should not be given.

Expected Actions
critical
Recognize compensated shock (tachycardia, cap refill >3s, dry mucosa, sunken fontanel)
Inclusion Criteria: Signs of poor perfusion (due to a medical cause) such as one or more of the following: Altered mental status; Delayed capillary refill (> 3 seconds); Decreased urine output; Respiratory rate greater than 20 breaths per minute in adults or elevated in children; Tachycardia or bradycardia for age, out of proportion to temperature; Weak, decreased or bounding pulses; Cool/mottled or flushed/ruddy skin.

critical

Pediatric: Fluid goal of up to a total of 60 mL/kg or 1 liter of isotonic fluid by giving 20 mL/kg of isotonic fluid by administering rapid boluses (for cardiogenic shock give 10 mL/kg boluses).
