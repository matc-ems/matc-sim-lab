↳ MATC: Hypothermia/Cold Exposure
critical
Measure core temperature (epitympanic or rectal) — the key assessment
If possible and reliable, EMS clinicians should perform core body temperature measurements and categorize patients into one of the three follow levels of hypothermia: Mild: 32.1°–35°C/89.8°–95°F; Moderate: 28.1°–32°C/82.5°–89.7°F; Severe: 24°–28°C/75.2°–82.4°F; Profound: less than 24°C (75.2°F). If neither esophageal nor rectal thermometers are available, an epitympanic field thermometer with an isolating ear cap may be used, but is generally less accurate.

important

Consider field-rewarming methods such as placement of large heat packs or heat blankets (chemical or electric if feasible) to the anterior chest or wrapped around the patient's thorax if large enough. Improper Care Branch
Atropine/pacing attempted
Trigger: Crew jumped to symptomatic-bradycardia treatment (atropine 1 mg IV and/or transcutaneous pacing) without checking core temperature or considering hypothermia. In a cold heart, atropine is largely ineffective and aggressive handling/pacing can trigger ventricular ectopy or VFib. Mistake is recoverable if the crew now recognizes the cause and pivots to rewarming.
Crew has anchored on the bradycardia as a primary cardiac problem and attempted atropine and/or transcutaneous pacing without first measuring core temperature or recognizing cold exposure. The cold myocardium does not respond to atropine. Pacing causes the patient discomfort but does not capture reliably. The intervention has not changed the underlying problem and may have precipitated an arrhythmia.
