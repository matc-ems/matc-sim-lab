Expected Actions
critical
Recognize heat stroke: hot, dry, anhidrotic skin + AMS + core >104°F
Heat Stroke: occurs when the cooling mechanism of the body ceases due to temperature overload and/or electrolyte imbalances. Patient core temperature is usually greater than 104°F. When no thermometer is available, it is distinguished from heat exhaustion by altered level of consciousness, seizures, or coma.
↳ MATC: Hyperthermia/Heat Exposure
critical
Move patient to cool area; shield from sun and heat source
Treatment and Interventions: Move victim to a cool area and shield from the sun or any external heat source.
↳ MATC: Hyperthermia/Heat Exposure
critical
Remove clothing; loosen restrictive garments
Treatment and Interventions: Remove as much clothing as is practical and loosen any restrictive garments.
↳ MATC: Hyperthermia/Heat Exposure
critical
Confirm core temperature (rectal or tympanic); document 105.4°F
Vital signs: Core temperature: usually 104°F or greater (if thermometer available); Skin: flushed and hot, dry or sweaty, signs of first or second degree burns from sun exposure.
↳ MATC: Hyperthermia/Heat Exposure
critical
Begin active cooling NOW: ice packs to neck/groin/axillae + mist + fan (or ice-water immersion if available)
If core temperature is greater than 104°F (40°C) or if altered mental status is present, begin active cooling by: Ice bath immersion provides the most rapid cooling mechanism. If ice bath immersion is not available, consider the following: Tarp-assisted cooling with oscillation; Rotating ice water-soaked towels or sheets; Continually misting the exposed skin with tepid water while fanning the victim; Truncal ice packs may be used, but are less effective than evaporation.
