important
Place in lateral recovery position; pad head; keep suction at the head
Patient positioning can significantly impact respiratory mechanics. Elevating the head or padding (shoulders, occiput) can assist with opening airway and respiratory mechanics. This can both improve the ability to ventilate and limit aspiration.
↳ MATC: Airway Management (Includes RSI)
critical
Insert OPA (no gag reflex); prepare two-person BVM
Oropharyngeal airways (OPA) or nasopharyngeal airways (NPA) can be placed if needed to maintain a patent airway and make BVM ventilation more effective. OPA are used for patients without gag reflex. Two-person, two-thumbs-up BVM ventilation is preferred.
↳ MATC: Airway Management (Includes RSI)
critical
Two-person BVM with PEEP 5 cmH2O, 10–12 breaths/min; titrate to SpO2 94–98%
Use bag-valve-mask (BVM) ventilation in the setting of respiratory failure or arrest. PEEP should be used with BVM. 5 cmH2O is generally an appropriate initial PEEP setting. Tidal volume: Ventilate with just enough volume to see chest rise, approximately 6–8 mL/kg ideal body weight. Rate Adult: 10–12 breaths/minute.

critical

Treatments and Interventions: If the patient has respiratory depression from a confirmed or suspected opioid overdose, consider naloxone administration. The administration of the initial dose or subsequent doses can be incrementally titrated until respiratory depression is reversed. The administration of naloxone can be titrated until adequate respiratory effort is achieved if administered with a syringe IV, IM, IN, or ETT.

Improper Care Branch
Naloxone first — aspirated
Trigger: Crew administered naloxone before adequate airway management. Approximately 60 seconds after naloxone, patient took a sudden deep gasp and aspirated visible vomit. SpO2 dropped from 78% to 82%, RR rose to 24 and labored. Patient is now awake-but-altered, combative, swatting at hands, while bilateral coarse breath sounds confirm aspiration.
Judgment-error branch. Crew gave naloxone (most commonly 2 mg IN or 0.4 mg IV) WITHOUT first suctioning the oropharynx or establishing controlled ventilation. The patient roused suddenly while vomit was still pooled in the posterior pharynx and aspirated a substantial volume on his first deep breaths. SpO2 has dropped to 82% on room air despite the airway being open. New coarse crackles and rhonchi heard bilaterally — aspiration pneumonitis is brewing. He is now altered AND combative AND hypoxic, making suction far harder than it would have been 90 seconds ago.
