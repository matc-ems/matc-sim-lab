Initial Contact
EMS arrives at the bookstore back office to find a 24-year-old female sitting in a desk chair, alert, anxious-appearing, breathing rapidly and deeply. Coworker reports a possible panic attack. The picture is deceptive: respirations are FAST and DEEP (Kussmaul), not shallow, and SpO2 is normal at 99% — she is not actually hypoxic or short of breath in the traditional sense. She has Type 1 diabetes with an insulin pump, skipped her overnight basal, polyuria/polydipsia x2 days, mild abdominal pain, faintly fruity breath, and a MedicAlert bracelet visible. This is the assessment window. The teaching point: a complete primary survey, targeted history, breath odor, and a FINGER-STICK GLUCOSE — not a paper bag — is what discriminates DKA from panic attack. Improper Care Branch
Treated as panic attack; acidosis worsening
Trigger: Crew accepted dispatch's 'panic attack' label, coached the patient through slow breathing or had her breathe into a paper bag to rebreathe CO2, and did not obtain a finger-stick glucose. Compensatory hyperventilation is reversed, PaCO2 rises, arterial pH falls, and the metabolic acidosis worsens.
Crew accepted the dispatch label and the surface presentation. They coached slow breathing or had the patient breathe into a paper bag to 'rebreathe CO2.' No finger-stick glucose was checked. Coaching/rebreathing has the opposite of the intended effect: it raises the patient's PaCO2, which removes her compensatory hyperventilation, drops her arterial pH further, and worsens the metabolic acidosis. Over a few minutes she becomes more lethargic, her speech slows, her respirations become slightly more shallow (no longer fully compensating), she vomits once, and her mental status deteriorates from GCS 15 to GCS 12 (E3 V4 M5). This is the wrong-turn branch — the teaching point is that anchoring on a dispatch label without doing the assessment causes harm.
