Expected Actions
critical
Recognize multi-system anaphylaxis (skin + airway + GI + hypotension)
Anaphylaxis – More severe and is characterized by an acute onset involving: The skin (urticaria) and/or mucosa with either respiratory compromise or decreased BP or signs of end-organ dysfunction OR Hypotension for that patient after exposure to a known allergen OR Two or more of the following occurring rapidly after exposure to a likely allergen: Skin and/or mucosal involvement; Respiratory compromise (dyspnea, wheezing, stridor, hypoxemia); Persistent gastrointestinal symptoms (vomiting, abdominal pain, diarrhea); Hypotension or associated symptoms.
↳ MATC: Anaphylaxis and Allergic Reaction
critical
Epinephrine 0.3 mg IM (1 mg/mL) anterolateral thigh IMMEDIATELY
Epinephrine administration is the primary treatment for anaphylaxis. If signs of anaphylaxis, administer epinephrine 1 mg/mL at the following dose and route: Adult (25 kg or more) 0.3 mg IM in the anterolateral thigh.
↳ MATC: Anaphylaxis and Allergic Reaction
critical
Allow tripod/upright position — do NOT force supine with airway swelling
Patient positioning can significantly impact respiratory mechanics. Patients with severe bronchospasm should be left in the position of comfort (perhaps tripod) whenever possible. Elevating the head or padding (shoulders, occiput) can assist with opening airway and respiratory mechanics.

Biphasic reaction
Trigger: Biphasic anaphylactic reaction — return of symptoms ~12 minutes after initial improvement despite continued bronchodilators and steroids. Mechanism: ongoing mast cell mediator release outlasting the first epi dose.
Approximately 12 minutes after the first IM epinephrine dose, en route to the ED, the patient deteriorates again. Stridor returns and intensifies, voice becomes more muffled, expiratory wheeze worsens, SpO2 drops from 96% to 92%, BP falls from 102/64 to 88/56, HR climbs back to 128. This is a classic biphasic anaphylactic reaction — a return of symptoms hours (or, here, minutes) after initial improvement, often with similar or worse severity. Crew must give a SECOND IM epi 0.3 mg, continue fluid resuscitation, and consider an epinephrine infusion if symptoms remain refractory. Reassess airway plan — if obstruction climbs back toward 60+ with worsening voice and stridor, the window for intubation is narrowing and surgical airway preparation should be confirmed.

Improper Care Branch
IV first, Epi delayed
Trigger: IV access attempted before IM epinephrine. 3-4 minute delay in time-to-epinephrine. Airway edema progresses; obstruction climbs from 60 toward 85+. Patient transitions from talking to silent.
Crew prioritized establishing IV access before giving IM epinephrine, planning to administer epi IV instead of IM. Two venipuncture attempts on a hypotensive, vasoconstricted, anxious patient take 3-4 minutes. During that delay, the airway edema progresses unchecked: stridor becomes louder and now biphasic with both inspiratory and expiratory components, voice becomes nearly inaudible, lip and tongue swelling worsen visibly, SpO2 drops from 91% to 85%, and the patient transitions from anxious-talking to gasping and silent. This is the classic anaphylaxis trap — treating it like septic or hypovolemic shock (line first, then drugs) instead of like the time-critical mast cell event it is. The fix is RESCUE IM epi NOW, then manage a much-more-difficult airway. This branch teaches that IM epi precedes everything else, including IV access.
