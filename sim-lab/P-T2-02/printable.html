critical
Recognize compensated shock: tachycardia, cap refill >3 sec, AMS, oliguria
Inclusion Criteria: Signs of poor perfusion (due to a medical cause) such as one or more of the following: Altered mental status; Delayed capillary refill (> 3 seconds); Flash capillary refill (> 1 second) seen in early septic shock; Decreased urine output; Respiratory rate greater than 20 breaths per minute in adults or elevated in children; Hypotension for age... Tachycardia or bradycardia for age, out of proportion to temperature; Weak, decreased or bounding pulses; Cool/mottled or flushed/ruddy skin
↳ MATC: Shock
critical
Recognize BP is misleading: peds compensate until late hypotensive collapse
Key Considerations: In most adults, tachycardia is the first sign of compensated shock, and may persist for hours. Hypotension indicates uncompensated shock, which may progress to cardiopulmonary failure within minutes. Hypotension is a late and ominous sign in pediatric uncompensated shock

Improper Care Branch
Reassured by BP, no fluids
Trigger: Crew was falsely reassured by a 'normal-for-age' BP of 92/58 in the face of clear compensated-shock criteria (tachycardia 168, capillary refill 4 sec, AMS, oliguria, dry mucous membranes, mottled skin). They forwent IV access and fluid resuscitation. Mid-transport the patient transitioned from compensated to decompensated shock, which in peds is sudden and severe.
Crew sees BP 92/58 and decides the child is 'sick but stable.' They place a non-rebreather, get a perfunctory set of vitals, and load for routine transport WITHOUT establishing IV access, WITHOUT giving a fluid bolus, and WITHOUT advance notification. Tachycardia is dismissed as 'fever' or 'crying.' Roughly 6–8 minutes into transport — exactly the pediatric pattern — the child crashes. He stops responding even to painful stimulus, BP drops precipitously to 64/40, HR climbs further then becomes irregular, and capillary refill is now >5 seconds with mottling to mid-thigh. Crew is now scrambling for IO access in a moving rig under far worse conditions than they had on scene. This phase is the demonstration of why a 'normal' BP in a tachycardic, mottled, oliguric child is NOT reassuring — it is the calm before the cliff.
