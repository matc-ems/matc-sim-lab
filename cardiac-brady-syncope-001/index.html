Branch - Skip Atropine, Pace Immediately
Crew bypassed atropine and went directly to TCP without trial
The crew elected to bypass atropine and proceed directly to transcutaneous pacing. While protocol allows direct TCP for impending hemodynamic collapse, this patient has time for an atropine trial. Direct TCP is not necessarily wrong - it just adds discomfort and need for sedation. The case becomes a discussion point for the debrief: atropine is cheap, fast, and may avoid TCP altogether in mixed mechanism bradycardias. In patients who clearly will not respond (e.g., transplant heart, agonal patient with capture window closing), direct TCP is preferred.

Learning Objectives
  • Define symptomatic bradycardia: HR < 60 with symptoms (AMS, chest pain, syncope, shock, diaphoresis) or hemodynamic instability
  • Apply the stepwise Bradycardia protocol: airway/O2/monitor/12-lead/IV/glucose first, then atropine, then chronotropes or TCP
  • Identify Third Degree (Complete) Heart Block on the 12-lead and understand why atropine often fails (block is at AV node; atropine works above the block)
  • Recognize that the protocol explicitly recommends moving 'quickly to chronotropic agents or transcutaneous pacing' for third-degree block or denervated heart (e.g., transplant)
  • Differentiate electrical capture (pacer spike followed by wide QRS) from mechanical capture (palpable pulse synchronous with pacer); use the femoral, not the carotid, to confirm mechanical capture
  • Set pacing output 10-20 mA above the capture threshold for safety margin
  • Provide sedation (midazolam) and analgesia (fentanyl) for any patient receiving TCP
  • Recognize when to escalate to chronotropic infusion or push-dose epinephrine if pacing fails or BP remains low
  • Identify the tox/drug-induced bradycardias (beta-blockers, calcium channel blockers, digoxin, clonidine) where treatment differs
