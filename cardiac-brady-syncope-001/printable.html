Initial Presentation - Symptomatic Bradycardia After Syncope
Crew arrives to find a 74-year-old female who briefly lost consciousness and is now persistently bradycardic (HR low 30s), hypotensive (SBP 80s), and symptomatic with weakness, lightheadedness, vague chest pressure, and shortness of breath. The crew must recognize this as symptomatic bradycardia with hemodynamic instability - the patient meets the criteria for the Bradycardia protocol. The 12-lead ECG reveals third-degree (complete) heart block with a wide ventricular escape rhythm. This is a presentation where atropine is unlikely to work because the AV node is blocked - the protocol specifically calls out that third-degree block may not respond to atropine and may require chronotropic agents or pacing.

Improper Care Branch
Branch - Skip Atropine, Pace Immediately
Trigger: Crew bypassed atropine and went directly to TCP without trial
The crew elected to bypass atropine and proceed directly to transcutaneous pacing. While protocol allows direct TCP for impending hemodynamic collapse, this patient has time for an atropine trial. Direct TCP is not necessarily wrong - it just adds discomfort and need for sedation. The case becomes a discussion point for the debrief: atropine is cheap, fast, and may avoid TCP altogether in mixed mechanism bradycardias. In patients who clearly will not respond (e.g., transplant heart, agonal patient with capture window closing), direct TCP is preferred.
