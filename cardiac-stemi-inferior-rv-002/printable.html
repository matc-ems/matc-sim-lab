Initial Contact
EMS arrives to find a 57-year-old male seated in an office chair at his workplace, pale and diaphoretic, with substernal chest pressure radiating to the right shoulder and through the back. He is alert but lightheaded; SBP is borderline at 90, HR is bradycardic at 54 with regular rhythm — the classic 'inferior MI with RV involvement' physiology. The teaching point centers on 12-lead interpretation: ST elevation in II, III, aVF with III > II and reciprocal depression in I and aVL points to an RCA culprit, and right-sided V4R confirms RV involvement. The treatment pivot is 'fluid first, careful with nitrates.'

Expected Actions
critical
Primary survey; recognize hypotensive bradycardic chest pain as possible inferior STEMI + RV
Inclusion Criteria: Chest pain or discomfort in other areas of the body (e.g., arm, jaw, epigastrium) of suspected cardiac origin, shortness of breath, associated or unexplained sweating, nausea, vomiting, or dizziness. May also present with CHF, syncope, and/or shock. Assess the patient's cardiac rhythm and immediately address pulseless rhythms, symptomatic tachycardia, or symptomatic bradycardia.

critical

Patient Safety Considerations: Consider placing defibrillator pads on high-risk patients.
↳ MATC: Chest Pain/Acute Coronary Syndrome (ACS)/STEMI
critical
12-lead EKG within 10 min; look at II, III, aVF; III > II suggests RCA
The 12-lead EKG is the primary diagnostic tool that identifies a STEMI; it is imperative that EMS clinicians routinely acquire a 12-lead EKG within 10 minutes for all patients exhibiting signs and symptoms of ACS.

important

The location of the infarct does not preclude the use of nitrates. Right-sided leads are of no additional value if an inferior STEMI has been diagnosed and such findings (presumed RV infarct) do not preclude the use of nitroglycerin: however, continually monitor the patient's hemodynamic status and be prepared to resuscitate if hypotension occurs.

Improper Care Branch
NTG given without fluid first → crash
Trigger: Nitroglycerin administered before fluid resuscitation in a patient with inferior STEMI and presumed RV involvement, causing precipitous hypotension.
Crew gave nitroglycerin to a hypotensive RV-infarct patient before any fluid resuscitation. The preload-dependent RV cannot maintain stroke volume against further venodilation. Within 90 seconds BP crashes to 60/36 and the patient becomes confused. Crew must stop NTG, drop patient supine with legs elevated, give rapid IV fluid bolus, and prepare push-dose pressor.
