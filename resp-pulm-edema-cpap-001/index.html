CPAP applied without NTG, OR NTG withheld > 5 minutes despite SBP >= 160 mmHg
CPAP was applied but nitroglycerin was either never given or was withheld for more than 5 minutes despite SBP >= 160 mmHg. Without preload reduction, continued fluid shift overwhelms alveolar recruitment from CPAP alone. The patient deteriorates: worsening hypoxia, climbing EtCO2, fatigue, and declining mental status progressing toward frank respiratory failure. Crew must now escalate airway management (RSI) and give NTG emergently.

Learning Objectives
  • Identify the clinical features that distinguish acute cardiogenic pulmonary edema from COPD exacerbation, pneumonia, and asthma
  • Describe the pathophysiology of acute pulmonary edema - elevated LV filling pressures cause fluid to move into the alveoli; it is a distribution problem, not total-body fluid overload
  • Apply the Respiratory Distress protocol nitroglycerin dose-scaling rule: 0.4 mg SL for SBP < 160 mmHg and 0.8 mg SL for SBP >= 160 mmHg or MAP > 120
  • Verbalize the absolute contraindication to nitrates within 48 hours of phosphodiesterase-5 inhibitors (sildenafil, tadalafil, vardenafil) and with IV epoprostenol or treprostinil
  • Explain why CPAP works in APE - it is not just oxygenation; positive pressure decreases venous return / preload, redistributes lung water, and recruits partially collapsed alveoli
  • Explain why prehospital furosemide is contraindicated per protocol Note #6 - APE is a distribution problem, and inappropriate diuresis can cause harm, especially in mimicking conditions like pneumonia or sepsis
  • Describe the critical rule of CPAP continuity - do NOT break the mask seal until the hospital circuit is ready at the bedside
  • Recognize impending respiratory failure patterns - rising EtCO2 with tachypnea, declining mental status, fatigue with slowing RR - and escalate to RSI
