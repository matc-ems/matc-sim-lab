  • Hypoxic PEA arrest — etiology is bronchospasm/hypoxia, NOT primary cardiac
  • Invasive airway placement in severe bronchospasm carries HIGH RISK: air trapping → auto-PEEP → pneumothorax → further CV collapse
  • If invasive airway is used, ventilate SLOWLY with LONG expiratory times (e.g., 6–8/min with I:E ratio 1:4 or longer)
  • Give IM epinephrine (or IV epinephrine per cardiac arrest guideline) AND continue aggressive bronchodilator therapy — reversing bronchospasm is the only way out
  • Consider needle decompression if breath sounds become asymmetric or hemodynamics deteriorate further (tension pneumothorax risk)
Expected Actions
CRITICAL Initiate high-quality pediatric CPR — compressions at 100–120/min, depth 1/3 chest AP diameter, 15:2 ratio with two rescuers
CRITICAL Provide BVM ventilation with SLOW, LOW-VOLUME breaths (just enough to see chest rise, ~6–8 mL/kg = 150–200 mL) and LONG expiratory time to avoid breath stacking; rate 8–10/min during arrest
CRITICAL Administer Epinephrine 0.01 mg/kg IV/IO of 0.1 mg/mL (1:10,000) concentration = 0.25 mg (2.5 mL) IV/IO push every 3–5 minutes per pediatric cardiac arrest dosing; flush with 5 mL NS
CRITICAL Continue nebulized Albuterol 5 mg inline with BVM (adapter) or via ETT if intubated; this is a bronchospastic arrest — treating the cause is essential
CRITICAL AVOID rushing to endotracheal intubation — use BVM with two-person technique first. Learning Objectives
  • Recognize 'quiet chest' / 'silent chest' as a sign of near-fatal asthma — absence of wheezing in a severely dyspneic asthmatic means air is NOT moving, not that bronchospasm has resolved
  • Identify the risk factors for fatal asthma per protocol: prior ICU admission, prior intubation, repeated ED visits, heavy beta-agonist use, medication noncompliance
  • Understand indications for IM epinephrine in asthma: impending respiratory failure with no improvement on nebulized bronchodilators
  • Articulate why invasive airway placement is dangerous in severe bronchospasm: positive pressure causes air trapping, auto-PEEP, pneumothorax, and cardiovascular collapse
  • Apply pediatric weight-based dosing accurately: epinephrine 0.01 mg/kg IM (max 0.3 mg), methylprednisolone 2 mg/kg (max 125 mg), magnesium 40 mg/kg (max 2 g)
  • Distinguish rescue therapy (bronchodilators, epinephrine) from adjuncts (steroids act over hours; magnesium is supportive) — steroids and magnesium do NOT rescue the crashing patient
