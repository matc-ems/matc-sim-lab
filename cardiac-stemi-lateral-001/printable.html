SAMPLE History
S Signs & Symptoms
71-year-old female, retired schoolteacher, called her daughter complaining of a dull aching pain in her left jaw and the side of her neck that started while she was watering her garden. She thought it might be a tooth or sinus problem. Over the next 90 minutes she became progressively nauseated, started sweating despite cool temperature, and felt 'just exhausted, like I have the flu.' Daughter arrived, found her pale and clammy, and called 911. Patient repeatedly minimizes her symptoms — 'it's probably just my tooth' — and denies chest pain when asked directly. On further questioning admits to a vague pressure 'across the chest' but says it doesn't really hurt. Has been short of breath walking from the kitchen to the couch. She has a history of hypertension, well-controlled type 2 diabetes, and hyperlipidemia, and takes a baby aspirin daily.

Initial Contact
EMS arrives to find a 71-year-old female seated in her recliner. The dispatch comment ('flu-like, jaw pain') and the patient's own minimization ('it's just my tooth') belie an obviously diaphoretic, pale, mildly dyspneic woman who has been ill for 2 hours. The teaching point here is the AHA-recognized atypical female-pattern ACS presentation: jaw/neck pain, fatigue, nausea, diaphoresis, mild dyspnea — without overt chest pain. She must be evaluated as a possible ACS patient and a 12-lead must be obtained early.

Expected Actions
critical
Primary survey; recognize atypical ACS in elderly diabetic female
Inclusion Criteria: Chest pain or discomfort in other areas of the body (e.g., arm, jaw, epigastrium) of suspected cardiac origin, shortness of breath, associated or unexplained sweating, nausea, vomiting, or dizziness. Atypical or unusual symptoms are more common in women, the elderly, and diabetic patients. May also present with CHF, syncope, and/or shock.

Improper Care Branch
Treated as flu/dental — no 12-lead
Trigger: Atypical female ACS presentation accepted as 'flu / dental' without a directed cardiac history or 12-lead; STEMI missed in the field with delayed reperfusion.
Crew accepts the patient's framing of 'just the flu and a sore tooth' and does not pursue a directed cardiac history or acquire a 12-lead EKG. Patient is loaded for transport as a 'sick person' to the closest community ED rather than a PCI center. STEMI is missed in the field, no STEMI alert is called, and reperfusion is delayed by the receiving ED having to discover the diagnosis themselves. This branch is the classic 'female-pattern MI miss' — a recognized public health and EMS quality issue.
