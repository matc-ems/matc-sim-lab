Initial Contact
EMS finds a 68-year-old man in obvious cardiogenic shock: pale, diaphoretic, cool extremities, sitting forward with frothy pink sputum, and unable to speak more than 1–2 words. Initial monitor shows sinus tachycardia at 118 with ST elevation in V1–V4 — anterior STEMI. BP is 78/52, lungs reveal rales to the mid-fields, and SpO2 is 86% on room air. This is cardiogenic shock with pulmonary edema — the protocol pathway is FUNDAMENTALLY DIFFERENT from straight septic or hypovolemic shock: a SMALL test fluid bolus (250 mL), early vasopressor support, CPAP for the pulmonary edema, and STEMI activation. Aggressive crystalloid here will worsen pulmonary edema. Nitrates are CONTRAINDICATED with this BP. The teaching point is recognition of the pump failure pattern + restraint with fluids + early pressor.

Improper Care Branch
Aggressive 2 L bolus given
Trigger: Aggressive 2 L crystalloid resuscitation precipitated worsening pulmonary edema and falling BP — classic cardiogenic-shock fluid trap; protocol requires withholding further fluid and pivoting to pressor + ventilatory support.
Crew anchored on a generic 'shock = fluids' approach and pressure-infused 2 L of NS without recognizing the cardiogenic pattern (cool/mottled, rales, frothy sputum, anterior STEMI). Within minutes the patient's pulmonary edema worsens dramatically — SpO2 falls into the 70s, rales rise to the apices, frothy pink sputum increases, and BP actually FALLS as the failing left ventricle decompensates further. The MATC Shock protocol explicitly states: 'If the patient condition deteriorates after fluid administration, rales or hepatomegaly develop, then consider cardiogenic shock and withholding further fluid administration.' The crew must STOP fluids immediately, escalate to CPAP/BVM with PEEP, start a vasopressor, and prepare for possible airway intervention if respiratory failure progresses.
